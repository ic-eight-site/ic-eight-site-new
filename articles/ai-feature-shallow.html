Why Your AI Feature Feels Shallow After the First Try

Most AI features fall into three categories. Only one of them creates a durable reason to come back. · Published 2026

Lately I've been using a few different AI Lens-style features — the kind that let you point a camera at something and get a response back. The first time, it's fun. By the third time, it feels thin.

A photo of an outfit gets "70s crochet vibes." A photo of dinner gets "the comfort of a Japanese winter." A photo of a cat gets its breed. None of this is wrong. It's just not useful enough to come back for. You could get the same thing from a search engine, or Pinterest, or a friend with decent taste.

Looking closer, I think many AI features fall into three broad categories. They can all be useful, but only some create a durable reason to come back.

1. Task features

Translate this menu. Read this label. Identify this plant. These are genuinely convenient, but they're also things Gemini or ChatGPT already do, in a browser tab you already have open. Convenience alone isn't a reason to open a separate app — it's a reason to open whichever app is already in front of you.

2. Interpretive features

This is the interpretive version of AI Lens: point a camera at something and get back a story, a style read, a cultural interpretation, or a clever caption. Here, the value depends almost entirely on what you choose to point it at. Aim it at a genuinely interesting object — a striking piece of furniture, a distinctive photograph — and the AI can say something worth reading. Aim it at an ordinary box of tissues, and it produces the same enthusiastic sentence anyway, because that's what it's built to do.

That's because not every input has a story built into it. Art is dense with intention — someone made a choice, and there's something real to interpret. A plate of leftovers is not. Ask AI to interpret something with no interpretive depth, and it fills the gap with generic warmth. Generic warmth is exactly what makes a feature forgettable — and in this category, the feature's apparent intelligence is really just borrowing the user's own taste.

None of this means the category is weak. It's audience-dependent. For people who already notice and collect visual details, from art and architecture to old storefronts, street signs, ornamental details, book covers, and cultural references, being handed a clever read of what they captured can be genuinely rewarding. For someone casually photographing an ordinary lunch with no particular question behind it, the same feature falls flat. The category is not broken. It just needs to know who it is for.

3. Personal-tool features

This is the category that holds up. It doesn't ask "what is this," it asks "what can I do with this now" — and it does that by connecting to something the user already has: what's in your fridge, what's in your closet, what you've already read, what you did last time.

A useful version wouldn't just name the style of a shirt. It would look at what you already own, the weather, how far you're walking, and build an outfit out of it. It wouldn't estimate the calories in your dinner — it would notice what's already in your fridge and suggest what to cook with it, or gently point out that you still have that tofu from last week. The camera, or the photo, or the list of ingredients — none of it is the product. It's just one way of getting information in. The product is the connection to something the user is actually trying to do.

Even AI defaults to the crowd

Here's the part that surprised me. I assumed that using AI, instead of a plain recommendation algorithm, would automatically solve the personalization problem. It doesn't.

I once asked an AI for movie recommendations using only a title I liked. What came back shared surface features with it, but missed what I actually valued entirely — the kind of match you'd get from "people who liked this also liked that."

Then I tried again, differently. I described what I actually wanted: a story centered on someone who has quietly stopped believing he's the main character of his own life, no romance subplot, something "funny, and then quietly devastating." That time, it named the film exactly right — and the next one, and the one after that.

The difference wasn't that the AI got smarter between the two questions. It was that the second time, I gave it my intention instead of my inventory item. A title alone tells an AI almost nothing about why you liked something. Without that, even the most advanced model falls back on the same thing a basic recommendation engine does: what's statistically similar to what everyone else liked.

The real question

The question worth asking about any AI feature isn't "can the AI answer this?" It's "does this product give someone a reason to come back?"

Task features get replaced by whatever general AI tool is already open. Interpretive features only work as well as what the user chooses to point them at, and who that user is. Personal-tool features — the ones built on inventory and intention — are the only category where the product itself is doing something a general AI assistant can't casually replicate.

Most AI features get weaker the more of them you stack together, because each new one answers "what is this" one more time without ever reaching "what should happen next." The features that hold up over time are the ones built around memory and intention, not description.

If your AI feature gets a strong first reaction but weak repeat use, the issue is rarely the model. It's usually the role the feature is playing in the product.
