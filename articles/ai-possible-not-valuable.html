AI Made More Things Possible. It Didn't Make More Things Valuable.

IC Eight · Observation

AI can now do things that were impossible, or absurdly expensive, a few years ago. Turn a photo into a poem. Summarize a painting. Rewrite a sentence fifty different ways. Build a small explanation around almost anything you point a camera at.

That's impressive. But "it can do this" is only the first question. The harder ones come after: Is this worth doing? Would someone pay for it? And even if they would, does what they pay exceed what it costs to win and keep them?

AI answered the first question. It did nothing to answer the other three.

A camera app I looked at

I recently looked at a camera app built around a simple idea: point it at something, and it explains what you're looking at — stories, context, interpretation, on the spot.

The kind of praise it draws is easy to imagine: it turned a street mural into a five-minute art lesson, it skips the shopping links and tells you why something matters, it's a pocket curator for whatever you happen to be standing in front of.

I can believe all of that. I can imagine trying it, enjoying it, telling a friend.

What I can't imagine as easily is the next step: would I pay for it?

That's a different question entirely. A product can be interesting without being useful. Useful without being worth paying for. It can even go viral without producing much revenue. Buzz and willingness to pay are not the same signal — and AI has made the first one far easier to generate than the second.

What actually makes people pay

Most purchases create a clear before and after. A problem existed, now it doesn't. Something took two hours, now it takes ten minutes. Someone felt uncertain, now they feel more confident. Even a small act of generosity works this way. Someone needs something. I can help. I give them money, their situation changes a little, and I get the simple satisfaction of having helped.

Now compare that to: I saw a mural, an app told me something interesting about it. There may be people for whom that change is worth paying for. But the burden is on the product to find them and prove it — "people thought it was cool" is not evidence that they wanted it enough to pay.

AI made weak ideas easier to build

Before generative AI, plenty of product ideas died quietly because they were too expensive to build. A personalized story generated for every object a user photographs would have been calculated, weighed against likely demand, and shelved.

Now the barrier is almost gone. A model generates the story. A vision model identifies the object. The interface goes together fast. The demo works, and suddenly the product exists.

But lowering the cost of building something doesn't raise the value of the thing you built. It just means more things whose value was never established get built anyway.

That shifts the real bottleneck. When generation was expensive, the scarce resource was the ability to make something at all. When generation is cheap, the scarce resource becomes judgment: what's worth making, which capability creates a meaningful change, which one is just entertaining, and who will actually pay.

"Wouldn't it be nice" is not a business case

This isn't only a consumer AI problem. AI can personalize every message, generate a variant of every page, write a custom explanation for every user. It can. The question is what changes as a result. Does the user finish something they couldn't finish before? Does friction disappear? Does conversion move? Does someone save time, save money, or earn more?

If nothing meaningful changes, AI just made an unnecessary activity cheaper to perform. That's not innovation — it's cheaper activity.

AI expands what's possible. It doesn't define what's worth doing. As building gets easier, the judgment required before building becomes more important, not less. The question is no longer just "can we build this?" It's whether anyone meaningfully benefits, whether they'll pay for that benefit, and whether we can deliver it profitably.
