Maybe It Was Never About Japan

Two walls, checked and cleared. · Published 2026

For twenty years, the pitch for why a product struggled in Japan always had two legs: the language, and the culture. The localization industry sold the first explanation. The Japan GTM industry sold the second. Each came with its own diagnosis, and conveniently, its own solution.

The language explanation used to be fair. Localization was heavy lifting, done by vendors, reviewed by more vendors, and it showed. But that wall is mostly gone now. AI writes fluent, native-sounding Japanese — not flawless, but fluent enough that "unnatural language" stopped being a credible reason for churn.

One leg knocked out. The other — culture — is still standing.

Nemawashi before every decision. The world's highest quality bar. Security checklists no one can fully satisfy. No direct sales — go through a distributor, because nobody buys from a stranger. None of it is fabricated. Some of it is even true, some of the time.

But here's a test worth running on each one: if this weren't true, would the product actually die? Most of the time, the honest answer is no. These things are real, but they're rarely fatal.

What's fatal is the same everywhere. Nobody works hard to adopt something that isn't worth the effort. If the product doesn't solve a real problem, if there's no compelling reason to bother — that's not a Japan problem. That's a product problem, and it would sink the product anywhere.

Two walls, checked and cleared. Language: fine. Culture: real, but rarely fatal.

The real cost of these explanations was never about being wrong. It's that they give you somewhere else to look — anywhere but the one place that actually hurts to examine: your own product.

Maybe the answer was never in Japan at all. Maybe it was sitting in the product the whole time, waiting for someone to stop looking elsewhere.
